Resources for Investigators & Researchers

This page collects tools, guides, and reference material that I use when following the money, documenting fraud, or tracing how public programs break down for real people. Treat this as a living field kit – it will grow over time.

How to use this page

Start with the “Getting Oriented” section if you are newer to this work. If you already have a case, jump straight to “Case‑building & documentation”. When in doubt, bookmark this page and come back as your investigation evolves.

Nothing here is legal advice, financial advice, or a promise of coverage. It is a practical set of links and checklists to help you gather records, preserve evidence, and communicate clearly when you decide to submit a tip or report.

If you are in immediate danger, or someone is at risk of serious harm, please contact local emergency services first. This project cannot act as an emergency response line.

Getting oriented

Start here

Many people arrive here with a story that feels overwhelming: benefit cuts that make no sense, “overpayments” they cannot possibly repay, or paperwork that vanished into a black hole. Before you start uploading documents, it helps to sketch the basic shape of your situation.

A simple way to begin is to write three short paragraphs in your own words: what happened, who was involved (agencies, contractors, landlords, employers), and what changed for you when this started. You do not have to share names publicly to do this – it is for your own clarity.

Public records & data

Finding the paper trail

The strongest stories and cases usually have a mix of lived experience, paper records, and some kind of official data or correspondence. The links below are a starting point for finding that material in the United States.

  • State and local open‑records (FOIA/sunshine) laws
    Find the name of your state’s open‑records law and the agency that handles requests. Many state AG or press‑association sites maintain FOIA request templates.
    FOIA State agencies Paper trail
  • Benefits, case files, and notices
    Keep copies of every notice, denial letter, overpayment letter, recertification packet, and appeal decision. When possible, download PDFs from your online portal in addition to paper mail.
    SNAP / TANF Unemployment Disability
  • Court and docket systems
    Many counties and states publish searchable dockets for eviction, debt collection, criminal cases, and some administrative hearings. Search by your name and by agencies, landlords, or contractors involved.
    Evictions Debt Vendors
  • Regulator and watchdog databases
    Depending on the program, there may be inspector‑general reports, audit findings, or enforcement actions already published against agencies or contractors. These can help you understand if your case is part of a pattern.
    Audits Inspector general Patterns

Case‑building & documentation

Before you send a tip

You do not need a perfect case file to reach out. But a little structure will help any reporter, advocate, or lawyer understand what you are dealing with and what kind of follow‑up might actually help.

  • Timeline of what happened
    Write down key dates: when you applied, when benefits changed, when you were told something different over the phone, and when money was taken or threatened. Screenshots, portal messages, and letters all help anchor that timeline.
    Core step Screenshots Notices
  • Who touched your case
    Note every agency, office, or company name that appears on your paperwork or in your portal. This can include call‑center vendors, verification contractors, landlords, and debt‑collection firms.
    Agencies Contractors Vendors
  • Evidence bundle
    Create a folder (digital or physical) where you keep copies of everything you might later upload: letters, portal downloads, screenshots, photos of envelopes, and any notes from phone calls.
    Organizing Backup
  • What you need
    When you are ready to send a tip, note what you are hoping for: public attention, help understanding the rules, help finding legal aid, or simply a record that this happened to you.
    Expectations Next steps

Security, privacy & safety

Protecting yourself

Any time you are handling sensitive documents – especially IDs, Social Security numbers, or medical information – please move slowly and consider what you are comfortable sharing. You always have the right to blur, redact, or withhold certain details.

  • Basic digital‑safety habits
    Use strong, unique passwords and, if possible, turn on two‑factor authentication for your email and any state portals. Be careful using public Wi‑Fi when logging into benefits or banking accounts.
    2FA Email Portals
  • Redacting sensitive information
    Before you upload anything, consider covering full SSNs, full account numbers, or details about children and health conditions. In many cases, case numbers and dates are enough for pattern‑spotting without exposing everything.
    Redaction Case numbers
  • Offline copies
    Keep at least one offline copy of critical documents (on a USB drive or printed and stored safely), in case an account is locked or a portal goes down.
    Backup Resilience

Working with TruthDrop

Sending a tip

TruthDrop is the separate portal where you can submit tips, upload documents, and – over time – help map patterns across cases. This Resources page exists on The Vault site so you can prepare before you decide how much to share.

  • TruthDrop portal
    When the portal is live, you will be able to submit a tip and optionally attach documents. You can start by sharing your story in your own words and add files later if that feels safer.
    Coming soon Tip intake
  • What to include in a TruthDrop tip
    At minimum, explain what program or system failed, what you were told, and what concrete harm you are facing now (loss of housing, debt, food insecurity, etc.). You can note that you have documents without uploading them immediately.
    Program Harm Evidence
  • If you are not ready to submit
    You can use this page as a private checklist. Build your timeline, gather documents, and learn the landscape first. There is no requirement to send anything until you decide you want to.
    Preparation No pressure
